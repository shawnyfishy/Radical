                          RADICAL – Men’s Jewellery  
 
 
What problem are we solving? 
Men today lack meaningful identity symbols. 
In India, the search for meaning and internal structure among men is accelerating: over 65–70% of 
urban Indian Gen-Z and millennial men say they prefer brands aligned with personal values, not just 
price or status (BCG / Deloitte India consumer studies). At the same time, nearly 1 in 2 Indian men 
report stress linked to identity, career pressure, or lack of purpose.  
The global men’s jewellery market is growing, but it is dominated by decorative, trend-driven, 
and status-led products that fail to anchor discipline, purpose, or inner alignment.  
As a result: 
 Products are rotated, not retained 
 Emotional attachment is low 
 Repeat purchase is driven by novelty, not loyalty 
This creates a gap between: 
 external expression (what men wear), and 
 internal values (discipline, purpose, self-mastery) 
Men are spending more — but anchoring less. 
 
 
What’s the pain, friction, or unmet need? 
In a global consumer behavior study, 64% of male respondents agreed: 
“The things I buy reflect my identity and values.” 
Source: Harvard Business Review / Accenture Life Reimagined Consumer Study 
Jewellery fits perfectly into this category because: 
 It’s worn daily 
 It’s visible to self, even when not to others 
 It carries symbolic permanence 
In US, 78% of men think men’s jewellery is now mainstream. But, only 17–24% of men buy 
jewellery for themselves annually. In India, Over a quarter of men wear jewellery as a 
personal style statement, while many of them wear it primarily because it triggers emotions and 
adds sentimental value and meaning. 
The pain is lack of men’s jewellery products with meaning, experience and identity 
alignment, not lack of products.  
Men want: 
 symbols of strength without noise 
 masculinity without pretense 
 identity without validation 
But the market offers either: 
 fashion accessories with low emotional attachment and high churn, or 
 loud, exaggerated masculinity that feels performative and shallow 
There is no serious, philosophy-led option for men who value disciplined strength, clarity, and 
self-mastery. 
 
What’s your solution? 
RADICAL builds surgical-grade steel premium jewellery as identity wearables, not 
decoration. 
Each piece is designed as a Weapon for Identity — a physical reminder of discipline, purpose, 
and self-control. The jewellery is minimal, steel-driven, and rooted in Indian heritage and 
Warrior philosophy, designed to be worn daily and long-term. Throughout history warriors 
accepted steel as the final weapon material, RADICAL brings steel wearables for modern day 
warriors as a weapon for identity.

How is it different? 
RADICAL is story-driven, philosophy-led, not fashion-led. 
 Steel over precious metals (discipline and strength over indulgence, durable long term 
wear) 
 Meaning over ornamentation 
This approach increases: 
 Emotional attachment 
 Timeless design over trends, long-term wear cycles 
 Daily wear over rotation, repeat significance-based purchases 
RADICAL doesn’t sell appearance — it sells alignment with identity. 
 
Who is this for? 
Men aged 22–45 who: 
 value self-discipline and inner order 
 reject loud masculinity and shallow trends 
 want symbols that reflect who they are — and who they’re becoming 
Typically: 
 Founders, professionals, creators, athletes, disciplined thinkers 
 Urban, global, premium-oriented 
 Willing to pay for meaning and product quality, not discounts 
This is a psychographic segment, not a trend-following demographic. 
 
How big is the problem? 
Data-backed signals: 
 70%+ of urban Gen Z & millennial men globally say they prefer brands that reflect 
personal values, not just function or style. 
 Yet in men’s jewellery, <10% of brands lead with meaning or philosophy 
 Fashion-led jewellery sees high churn, with average wear cycles of weeks to a few 
months, not years 
 Global jewellery market: ~$350B+ 
 Men’s jewellery segment: ~$60–70B, growing at ~8–9% CAGR 
 India’s men’s jewellery market (becoming 3rd largest in the world): ~ ~$2.5–3B, growing 
faster than women’s jewellery in urban segments, still largely focused on gold/silver and 
fashion. 
RADICAL targets a high-margin, under-served niche within this market: 
identity-driven men’s jewellery, where emotional depth and long-term use matter more than 
trends. 
While men spend significantly on gadgets and apparel, less than 10–15% of daily-wear items are 
kept for emotional or symbolic reasons. 
This is a repeat, global problem, amplified by rising interest in purpose, self-mastery, and 
meaning among modern men. 
More importantly: 
 Identity-led products have higher LTV, lower churn, and lower discount dependency 
 Even a niche conservative capture (0.2%) of global demand supports a 1000 Cr brand 
over time 
 
 
Who are our competitors? 
RADICAL sits at the intersection of men’s jewellery, lifestyle, and identity — but no single 
competitor fully addresses this space. 
1. Fashion-led men’s jewellery brands 
Examples: Drip Project, Giva (men’s line), The Men Thing, Black Mamba, similar D2C 
labels 
 Market focus: trend, drops, visual appeal 
 Price range: ₹2,000–₹8,000 
 Strength: fast adoption, social visibility 
 Gap: low emotional stickiness, high rotation, weak identity anchoring 
These brands compete on style and relevance, not meaning. Products are replaced, not kept. 
 
2. Celebrity-backed lifestyle / jewellery brands 
Examples: MetaMan by Sunil Shetty 
 Market focus: grooming + jewellery + lifestyle 
 Backed by celebrity trust and mass appeal 
 Strength: reach and familiarity 
 Gap: broad positioning dilutes depth; jewellery remains an accessory, not an identity 
object 
These brands optimize for scale and accessibility, not long-term symbolic value. 
Unisex brands like Palmonas by Shraddha Kapoor (Trend driven) and Tayaani by Karan Johar 
(Aspirational) are not exclusively targeting masculine identity.  
 
3. Luxury jewellery brands (global & Indian) 
Examples: Tiffany & Co., Cartier, Tanishq (men’s pieces) 
 Market focus: status, craftsmanship, precious metals 
 Price range: ₹50,000–₹5,00,000+ 
 Strength: aspiration and heritage 
 Gap: not built for daily masculine wear; status signaling over personal discipline 
Luxury brands sell who you’ve become socially, not who you’re becoming internally. 
 
4. Masculinity & self-improvement brands (non-jewellery) 
Examples: fitness, coaching, grooming brands 
 Market focus: habits, performance, improvement 
 Strength: behavior change 
 Gap: no physical, permanent identity symbol 
They shape behavior — but don’t anchor identity through objects. 
 
 
 
Why none fully solve the problem 
Across categories: 
 ~90% of men’s jewellery brands are fashion-led 
 ~10% are luxury/status-led 
 ~0% are identity-led, philosophy-anchored, daily-wear symbols 
RADICAL occupies that white space.

One scrappy hack we’d use to get your first 1,000 customers 
I’d build the first 1,000 customers through identity-based distribution, not ads. 
Specifically: 
 Identify 10–15 high-discipline communities (founder circles, leadership cohorts, combat 
sports gyms, selective colleges, spiritual/meditative groups, etc.) 
 Offer a founder-only / cohort-only RADICAL edition (same core piece, limited 
engraving or mark) 
 Seed 50–100 pieces with influential members in each group (not influencers, but 
respected insiders) 
 Let the product spread through quiet curiosity, not promotion 
RADICAL isn’t something you “push” — it’s something people ask about. 
This approach keeps CAC low and ensures the right 1,000, not just any 1,000. 
 
If we had to live or die by one business metric, which would 
it be — and why? 
 
Repeat wear rate / regular usage rate. 
Not revenue. 
Not followers. 
Not conversion rate. 
If customers wear RADICAL daily, it proves: 
 the product has emotional stickiness 
 the symbol is doing its job 
 the brand has moved from accessory → identity 
A product worn daily has: 
 higher LTV 
 organic word-of-mouth 
 pricing power 
If RADICAL isn’t being worn regularly, it’s failing its core promise. 
 
If, after repeated iterations: 
 customers like the product 
 but don’t form emotional attachment 
 don’t wear it consistently 
 and don’t assign meaning to it 
—that’s the signal. 
RADICAL can survive slow growth. 
It cannot survive indifference. 
A pivot would preserve: 
 the insight (men want identity anchors) 
 but make changes the form factor  
 
If you had to cut one major feature to launch faster, what 
would you drop — and why? 
I would drop broad marketplace distribution at launch. 
Listing on large platforms early creates pressure to discount, chase reviews, and optimize for 
algorithms instead of meaning. For RADICAL, early adoption needs context and intent. I’d 
launch through owned channels and tight communities first, validate attachment and pricing 
power, and only expand distribution once the brand stands for something clear. 
Depth first. 
Breadth later. 
What’s our 90-day execution plan? 
Days 1–30: Foundation 
 Finalize 2-3 hero jewellery pieces,  
- Same core geometry, philosophy, material (steel) and meaning. 
- Different form factors (pendant, rings, bracelets) not different stories. 
 Lock manufacturing & unit economics (target: 65–70% gross margin) 
 Build brand assets (AI-led content, brand shoots and product visuals, landing page) 
 Set up preorder / waitlist 
Days 31–60: Market Validation 
 Seed first 100–200 pieces in high-discipline communities 
 Capture feedback on wear frequency, emotional attachment 
 Refine pricing, messaging, packaging 
 Begin content-led audience building 
Days 61–90: First Scale 
 Deliver first paid orders 
 Push toward 1,000 customers 
 Test institutional / group orders 
 Prepare capital & mentor conversations for next phase 
North Star metric: daily wear rate / repeat usage 
 
What are the biggest risks and how will we you mitigate 
them? 
1. Market risk: “Too niche” 
Mitigation: 
Start with a tight psychographic niche, expand later. Identity-led brands scale depth-first, not 
breadth-first. 
2. Execution risk: Over-philosophizing 
Mitigation: 
Hero-product focus, strict SKU discipline, weekly shipping milestones. 
3. Competitive risk: Trend brands copying the look 
Mitigation: 
Meaning, restraint, and philosophy can’t be copied quickly. RADICAL’s moat is symbolism + 
consistency, not aesthetics alone. 
4. Capital risk: Early cash burn 
Mitigation: 
Lean AI-led ops, low inventory risk, preorder and institutional orders to fund growth.
